# Behavioral Interview

## Format
While technical interviews are trying to find out how technically skilled you are, behavioral interviews focus on your communication skills. These are typically around 30 minutes. A big mistake that many people make is not practicing very hard for them. It is difficult to come up with a well-thought-out, concise answer on the spot.

## How to Practice
It may seem difficult to practice when you don’t know what kinds of questions they’ll ask, but most interviewers use very similar questions. For example, most interviewers start by saying something like “tell me about yourself” or “why are you applying to this company?” Write down a few of your main points and try to memorize them. For these, shorter is better. It's much harder to memorize a long speech than a few short bullet points. To come up with questions, I recommend using an AI like <a href="https://chatgpt.com/" target="_blank">ChatGPT</a>. Give the AI your resume and the position you’re applying to, and it will be able to give you several questions you are likely to see in an interview. Try getting someone to ask you these questions. Answering a person is much different from rehearsing alone. If you have no other option, record yourself to review your answers.

## Answer Length
You don’t want to make your answers too long, since they may cause your interviewer to lose focus. You also don’t want to make your answers too short since short answers typically don’t answer a question in its entirety. Aim for the sweet spot of around 60-90 seconds. When practicing, time yourself. If you take too long, you may have to remove some fluff, while if your answers are too short, add a little. 

## Body Language
Body language is just as important as what you’re saying. If offered, make sure to give a firm handshake while maintaining eye contact. During your interview, you want to appear and sound confident, but not egotistical. Be engaged and enthusiastic. Employers much prefer to see someone excited for the job. Smiling, when appropriate, looks great during an interview. Sit up straight and keep good posture throughout. 


## Stories
Most of the interview process is just storytelling. You want to relate most questions you are asked to a relevant experience. For your stories, use the <a href="https://capd.mit.edu/resources/the-star-method-for-behavioral-interviews/" target="_blank">STAR</a> method. The STAR method is useful since it directly shows impact and is easy to follow. A structured story will always be better than an unstructured one. I would recommend memorizing bullet points for each of the following stories. You can then rephrase these to answer the questions the interviewer actually asks.
- A time you were wrong and how you improved
- A time you have shown initiative
- A project you’re proud of
- A time you worked on a team
- A time you explained something technical to someone non-technical
